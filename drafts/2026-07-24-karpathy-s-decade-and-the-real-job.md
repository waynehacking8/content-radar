Andrej Karpathy said recently that it will take a decade to work through the real issues with agents, and I think that's the most useful sentence anyone's said about this space in months. Not because it's pessimistic, but because it reframes what the actual work is. The demo, the impressive multi-step task completed in a slick video, was never the hard part. The hard part is everything after: evals that catch silent failures, recovery paths when a tool call returns something unexpected, and the slow accumulation of trust that lets a team hand an agent real responsibility instead of a scripted showcase.

That gap between "it worked in the demo" and "it's dependable enough to run unattended in production" is not a rounding error, it's the job. It's also, increasingly, the actual job description for solutions and forward-deployed engineering roles: not building flashier agents, but closing that gap for one customer's messy, specific environment at a time. If someone tells you agents are basically solved, ask them what happens on the agent's worst day, not its best one.
