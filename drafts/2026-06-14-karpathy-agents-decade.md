Andrej Karpathy said recently that it will take a decade to work through the issues with agents. I think he's right, and the analogy he uses is clarifying: self-driving cars.

The demos looked solved in 2016. Eight years later, it's still geofenced, heavily supervised, and limited to specific domains. Agents have the same dynamic — narrow, well-scoped tasks work remarkably well today, and open-ended autonomous operation keeps hitting edge cases that are individually rare but collectively constant.

This isn't pessimism. It's a useful calibration for anyone building or buying agentic systems. The teams shipping real value right now are mostly doing one of two things: keeping the task scope very tight, or keeping a human close enough in the loop to catch failures cheaply. Neither is a workaround — they are the architecture.

The decade isn't wasted time. It's the unglamorous engineering work of mapping failure modes, building recovery mechanisms, and earning the trust that eventually justifies broader autonomy.
