There's a post circulating from a developer who says LLMs are quietly eroding their career—the interesting work is being automated, the remaining work is reviewing AI output, and the skills that took years to build feel suddenly less valuable. I've read it three times.

The anxiety is real. But I think the diagnosis is incomplete. What's being automated is code production. What's becoming more valuable is knowing what to build, how to decompose it, how to evaluate whether the output is correct, and how to integrate it into systems that actually have to work in production.

That shift doesn't devalue engineering—it changes which engineering skills are in demand. Understanding system boundaries, failure modes, data flow, and the trust assumptions your architecture makes doesn't get automated away. If anything, those become the job.

The engineers I've seen thrive aren't the ones who use AI the least or the most. They're the ones who got clear on what they're actually responsible for. The accountability layer is still deeply human.
