The best engineers I've worked with lately aren't the ones with the cleanest git history. They're the ones who can walk into a client's half-documented, duct-taped system and make something work by Friday. That's basically the Forward Deployed Engineer job description, and it's stopped being a consolation prize for people who "couldn't cut it" at a product company. Sierra's Natalie Meurer made a point recently that stuck with me: product engineering and forward-deployed engineering are converging, because shipping a feature and making that feature actually work inside someone else's messy reality are becoming the same skill. Most AI systems fail not because the model is bad, but because nobody adapted it to the client's actual data, workflows, and edge cases. That adaptation work used to be an afterthought. Now it's the differentiator. If you're deciding where to spend your next year leveling up, the ability to sit with ambiguity and ship inside constraints you don't control is worth more than another benchmark chase.
