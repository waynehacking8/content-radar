There's a rhetorical pattern in AI discourse worth naming: inevitabilism. The idea that LLM progress is a force of nature, not a set of choices — and that skepticism is just failure to accept reality.

I've run into this in customer conversations. "AI will replace X" gets stated as physics rather than policy. But replacement, adoption, and integration are decisions made by people with specific incentives. They're not gravity.

This framing matters because inevitabilism short-circuits useful thinking. Once an outcome is treated as inevitable, you stop asking whether it should happen and only ask how fast. That's a poor frame for anyone designing systems, where the real work is usually: does this work reliably for specific people in a specific context, under what conditions, and at what cost?

The engineers I respect most in this space are deeply skeptical — not of the technology's capabilities, but of the gap between capability in a demo and capability in production. That skepticism isn't resistance to progress. It's engineering rigor applied to claims.
